July 14, 1993 — Checkpoint Harlan, Lincoln County, Knox Exclusion Zone

By 0900, Vasquez had lost two posts.

Not lost in the way the word meant at the perimeter — nobody was dead, nobody had been overrun. Lost in the administrative sense, the institutional sense, the sense that meant someone above her pay grade had decided that the soldiers assigned to her sector were needed more urgently somewhere else. Specialist Hodge, pulled from the Sector C interior gate at 0740 for reassignment to the south perimeter reinforcement. Private Yates, pulled at 0830 from the east checkpoint, same reason, same lack of explanation beyond the order itself.

She stood at the east checkpoint now, covering the gap. The checkpoint was a folding table, a radio, and a view of sixty yards of interior fence separating the military side from the civilian blocks. On the other side of the fence, three hundred and some civilians were beginning the morning the way they'd begun every morning since July 10th — water station, ration line, the slow inventory of another day inside the wire. From here she could see the line forming at the distribution point, shorter than yesterday, the table behind it emptier than yesterday, the woman working it not meeting anyone's eyes the way the woman working it had not met anyone's eyes for two days.

Vasquez watched. She had been watching for five days — not as surveillance, not as oversight, but as the specific duty that her role required. She was the NCO responsible for the eastern civilian-military interface: the boundary, the buffer, the line between three hundred civilians who needed things and a military structure that had progressively less to give them. Security rotations on the interior fence. Supply coordination between the armory quartermaster and the civilian distribution points. The person soldiers reported to when something happened at the boundary, and the person civilians looked toward when they needed something that the boundary was supposed to provide.

It was a job that required her to face outward — toward the civilians — while the institution behind her faced inward, toward itself. She'd understood that architecture since day two. It hadn't bothered her then. An interface was a function, not a position. She managed the function.

It was bothering her now.

The morning's briefing had been twelve minutes. Captain Hale, the CO's adjutant, reading from a clipboard with the particular cadence of a man who was delivering information he didn't fully believe to people he couldn't fully look at. Force posture adjustments. Asset reallocation. Perimeter reinforcement priorities. The language was clean and procedural and it meant: we are pulling everything toward the center, and the things at the edges are going to be uncovered, and the things at the edges include the people on the other side of your fence.

Nobody said consolidation in the briefing. They didn't need to. The word had been in the air since she'd named it for Diane two days ago, and the air hadn't gotten any cleaner.

She keyed the radio. "Post Seven, this is Vasquez. Status."

Static. Then: "Post Seven, Ramirez. I'm here, Sergeant. It's just me."

"Copy. Maintain visual on the south fence line. I'll rotate to you at 1030."

"Roger."

She set the radio down. Post Seven was the interior checkpoint nearest the staging area — the vehicle yard, the gate to the perimeter road. Ramirez was twenty-two and had been at Harlan for three days and was now the only soldier covering a checkpoint that should have had three. The math was not ambiguous. By noon, if the consolidation continued at its current rate, she would have no posts left on the eastern sector. The fence would be unmanned. The interface would be a line with nobody on either side of it except the civilians, who would notice, and who would draw the correct conclusion from the noticing.

She picked up her sidearm from the table, holstered it, and walked toward the medical station.

*   *   *

The medical station smelled the way it always smelled — antiseptic over canvas over the particular sweetness of too many bodies in too little space. Diane was at the supply shelf, counting. Her hands moved with the mechanical precision of a woman who had been doing inventory since before any of this started, the count a ritual that kept the wall standing between the nurse and everything the nurse had seen.

Vasquez sat on the folding chair by the tent flap. The same chair. The same position. The third or fourth time she'd sat here this week, enough that it had become a thing — not scheduled, not discussed, just the cadence of a woman who had started crossing the line between her side and this side with a regularity that had its own meaning.

"Two posts down this morning," Vasquez said. "Hodge and Yates. Pulled south."

Diane didn't turn from the shelf. "How many does that leave you?"

"Three. By noon, maybe two." She paused. "The east sector's going to be open."

Now Diane turned. The diagnostic eye — the one that read people the way it read patients, by what they did and not what they said. She looked at Vasquez and Vasquez could feel the assessment landing, the read being taken, the precise measurement of what was underneath the operational language.

"How much time," Diane said.

Vasquez understood the question. Not how much time until the posts were gone. How much time until the thing the posts represented — the boundary, the institution, the promise that the fence was there for a reason — stopped meaning anything.

"I told you three or four days, two days ago." She rubbed the scar on her right knuckles with her thumb, the gesture she made when she was choosing words. "That was wrong. The perimeter situation is accelerating. There's traffic on the military frequencies about the main boundary — south, southwest. Civilian unrest, possibly armed confrontation. If the perimeter goes—"

"Then consolidation isn't three days out. It's today."

"It could be today."

The tent was quiet. Through the canvas, the sounds of the compound — voices, a vehicle on the interior road, the distant thrum of a generator that was running but shouldn't be trusted. The sounds of a place still performing the functions of a place.

"If it's today," Diane said, "we need to be ready."

"I know." Vasquez looked at her hands. "I wanted you to hear it from me before you hear it from the order."

Diane crossed the tent and sat on the edge of the examination table, which put her three feet from Vasquez with nothing between them. The institutional distance that had existed between them on day one — the NCO and the civilian nurse, the uniform and the scrubs — had been eroding since the antibiotics, since the first name, since the particular exchange of trust that happened between two competent women who recognized each other across a line neither of them had drawn.

"Elena."

Vasquez looked up.

"When it happens. Whatever it looks like." Diane held her eyes. "Don't be on the wrong side of this."

Vasquez held the look. The weight of it was specific — not a request, not an order. A statement. The kind of statement that contained a future in it, the kind that said: I know who you are and I know where this is going and I need you to get there.

"I won't be," Vasquez said.

She stood up. Checked the time. Checked the radio on her hip. Walked to the tent flap and paused.

"I'll find you," she said. "When it moves."

Diane nodded once. Vasquez left.

The walk back to the east checkpoint took four minutes. In those four minutes, Ramirez called on the radio to report that a third soldier had been pulled from Post Seven's sector. Vasquez acknowledged, adjusted the coverage map in her head, and kept walking. The gap was wider. The pace was faster. The word she'd named two days ago was no longer approaching.

It was here.

*   *   *

Morrow found out about the reassignment at 1100, which was when Sergeant First Class Kimball from Second Platoon appeared in the barracks corridor with a clipboard and the expression of a man executing an order he hadn't been consulted about.

"Sergeant Morrow. Your personnel are being redistributed under the consolidation directive. Effective immediately." Kimball read from the clipboard without looking up, which was its own kind of statement — not rudeness but the particular avoidance of a man who knew the paper in his hands was doing something to someone and preferred not to watch it happen. "Corporal Reyes, reassigned to Lieutenant Paulson's fire team, Alpha section. Specialist Cutler, reassigned to motor pool operations under Sergeant Vickers. Private Webb, holding detail, north sector barracks. You are to report to Sergeant Major Collins at the command block for reassignment by 1300."

Morrow stood in the corridor and received it. The way he received things — upright, still, the information entering the structure he'd built inside himself and being processed through that structure's architecture. Squad dissolved. People separated. Command assumed by strangers. Structure replaced by a different structure that didn't know his people's names or their capabilities or what they'd been through on the road between Fort Knox and this corridor.

"Copy," he said.

Kimball looked up from the clipboard. Held Morrow's eyes for half a second — the half-second in which the man behind the order acknowledged what the order was doing. Then he moved on to the next name on his list.

Morrow stood in the corridor. The barracks were half-empty — soldiers already reassigned, already moving to new positions, the institutional machinery chewing through its own components and reassembling them in configurations that looked functional on a clipboard and felt like nothing in the corridors where the people actually lived.

He went to the squad bay.

Shores' bunk was stripped. The blanket folded on the bare mattress, the footlocker open and mostly empty — personal effects gone, issued gear left behind. Dog tags on the pillow. Morrow picked them up. The chain was warm from sitting in the canvas heat, the metal discs carrying the information the Army thought was important about Private First Class Kenneth Shores: name, blood type, service number, religion.

The tags didn't say: twenty years old. Mother in Elizabethtown, twenty minutes outside the zone. Younger brother. Birthday July 12th that he'd told no one about and that had passed two days ago without acknowledgment. First posting. Eight months in.

Morrow had known since last night. He'd woken at 0400 for the latrine and the bunk was empty and the knowing had been instant — not surprise, not anger, the recognition of a thing that had been inevitable since the consolidation pulled the military back from the southern perimeter, from the direction of Elizabethtown, from the direction of everything Kenny Shores had left behind when the Army told him Knox Country was a temporary assignment.

He hadn't reported it. The absence would be noted eventually, logged as AWOL or desertion depending on who did the paperwork. Morrow would not be the one who noted it. Some absences were a dereliction. This one was a son.

He put the dog tags in his pocket. Stood in the squad bay with the stripped bunk and the empty footlocker and the knowledge that his squad — the four people who had been his responsibility since the convoy left Fort Knox — was now two gone and three about to be taken.

The structure that was water to him. The thing he needed the way some people needed certainty. Being drained by the same institution that had filled it.

He sat on his bunk. Looked at his hands. Thought about his daughter in Georgia, who did not know where her father was and could not be told because the phones had been dead for thirteen days and the distance between Harlan, Kentucky and Dothan, Georgia was a distance that might as well be measured in years instead of miles.

The gunfire started at noon.

*   *   *

Vasquez was at Post Seven when it began — the distant, layered percussion from the south that Jesse Hutchins had identified from the civilian side an hour earlier. From the military side, the sound was different only in what accompanied it: radio traffic, immediate and dense, the channels flooding with the particular cadence of a command structure responding to a crisis it had anticipated and could not contain.

She listened to the radio the way she listened to everything — for what the words said and for what the gaps between the words meant. Perimeter sections reporting contact. Multiple breach points along the southern boundary. Civilian unrest at the main checkpoint — the protest that had started that morning escalating into something that the military's response had escalated further. Shots fired. Shots returned. And underneath all of it, drawn by the sound the way they were always drawn by sound, the dead.

"All eastern sector personnel, consolidate to armory block. Repeat, all eastern sector posts are to be abandoned. Consolidate to the armory block immediately."

Vasquez looked at the radio. Looked at the fence. On the other side, the civilians were already reacting — she could see them moving, clustering, the body language of three hundred people who had heard the gunfire and were trying to understand what it meant for them. A man was at the fence, hands on the chain-link, looking through at her with the expression of someone who understood that the soldier standing at the checkpoint was the last visible evidence of the institution that was supposed to be keeping them alive.

She keyed the radio. "Copy. Vasquez moving to armory."

She didn't look back at the man at the fence. She couldn't afford to. Looking back was the kind of thing that turned a decision into a negotiation, and what was happening was not a negotiation. It was an order. She followed orders. She had followed orders for fifteen years and following them was the structure she stood on and the structure was what kept her upright.

She moved.

The next two hours were the machinery of collapse, and Vasquez was inside it.

At the armory block, the consolidation was organized chaos — soldiers arriving from abandoned posts, vehicles being loaded, the command staff issuing directives that contradicted directives from twenty minutes ago. Hold the north perimeter. No — reinforce the south interior fence. No — prepare for evacuation north, vehicles staged at the main gate. The chain of command was functioning the way a chain functions when too many links are under load: each individual connection held, but the whole thing whipped and twisted and the direction of pull changed faster than the people holding it could adjust.

She did her job. Directed soldiers to positions. Relayed orders she'd received to the people who needed them. Filed the mental report that would never be written: asset count declining, ammunition expenditure exceeding resupply, medical section overwhelmed, civilian sectors now fully unattended. The woman at the east fence — the distribution volunteer who wouldn't meet anyone's eyes — was somewhere on the other side of a boundary that no one was guarding anymore. The man with his hands on the chain-link was somewhere behind a fence that no longer meant anything.

At 1430, the perimeter broke.

She didn't see it. She heard it — the radio traffic shifting from urgent to something past urgent, a register she had heard once before in her career, during a training exercise that simulated a position being overrun, except the training exercise had a whistle that ended it and this did not have a whistle. Sections reporting breach. Infected through the southern fence in numbers that the reports couldn't quantify — dozens became I can't count them became silence on the channel, which was worse than any number.

The compound changed. The soldiers around her changed. The difference between a military force executing a difficult withdrawal and a military force experiencing a rout is not visible in the large movements — the vehicles still move, the orders still come, the rifles still point outward. The difference is in the faces. In the half-second between receiving an order and executing it, where the calculation happens: is this order going to get me killed, and if it is, do I follow it anyway? Vasquez had spent fifteen years on the side of the answer that said yes. The answer was getting harder.

At 1500, she went to the medical station.

The route took her across the interior yard, past the staging area, through the gap between the armory block and the tent rows. The gap was uncontrolled now — no checkpoints, no posts, the interior fence gates standing open because the soldiers who would have manned them were either at the perimeter or gone. She moved fast, sidearm drawn, the awareness running on the frequency that fifteen years had tuned to maximum sensitivity. Shapes at the tree line behind the compound. Shapes that moved wrong. Not close — two hundred yards, maybe, beyond the outer fence — but present, drawn inward by the gunfire, converging on the sound the way water converges on a low point.

The medical station was empty.

She knew it before she opened the tent flap — knew it by the absence of sound, the missing hum of a space that was occupied and working. She pushed through the canvas and stood in the doorway and looked at what was left.

The supply shelf. Diane's shelf — the one she'd organized by hand on her second day, bandages left, medications middle, instruments right. Disrupted. Not ransacked — selectively emptied, the gaps where specific items had been removed and the remaining items left in the disarray of someone packing what mattered under time pressure. The bandages half-gone. The medications shelf nearly empty — the amoxicillin, the ibuprofen, the antibiotics Vasquez had sourced from the armory all taken. The instruments untouched, because you only took instruments if you had a place to use them and wherever Diane was going didn't have that.

The intake sheet was still pinned to the post. Frank Briars' name. The medication schedule. The notes in Diane's compact handwriting that documented five days of doing medicine with almost nothing in a place where almost nothing was all there was.

Vasquez looked at the sheet. At the name. Frank Briars, whose arm infection she'd sourced the cephalexin for, whose wife Carol had brought him to the station every morning, whose daughter Amy was sixteen and quiet and had the kind of patience that meant she'd already been through something before any of this started.

The Briars had not made it out. She didn't know this yet. She would learn it later. For now, the name on the sheet was just a name, and the empty station was just a fact, and the fact was: they left. Diane left. The family left. The people she had been crossing lines for since the antibiotics were gone, and she was still inside the machine.

She checked the shelf. Behind the remaining bandages, folded once, a piece of paper. Torn from a notebook — the kind of notebook Diane used for patient notes, the lined pages, the specific weight of the paper. Vasquez unfolded it.

Diane's handwriting. Compact, level, unhurried even in what must have been a departure measured in minutes:

Elena —

We couldn't wait. I'm sorry. East on the county roads, away from the perimeter. We have a shortwave radio — 7.285 MHz, upper sideband. Listen for us when you can.

Find us.

— D

Vasquez read it twice. Folded it. Put it in her breast pocket, the same pocket where she kept her pen and her patrol notes and the small, functional things that a career NCO carried because carrying them was the job.

The station was quiet. Through the canvas, the sound of the compound — engines, radio traffic, a burst of gunfire close enough to be inside the wire, which meant the perimeter breach was no longer at the perimeter. The institution she'd given fifteen years to was contracting around her, pulling inward, shedding everything at its edges, and the edges were where the people were.

She'd told Diane she wouldn't be on the wrong side.

She buttoned the breast pocket. Drew her sidearm. Walked out of the medical station without looking back, and the direction she walked was not toward the armory block.

It was toward the motor pool.

*   *   *

Morrow disobeyed the order at 1620.

The order had come from Lieutenant Paulson, the officer who had absorbed what was left of Morrow's squad into a consolidated platoon that existed on paper and in Paulson's clipboard and nowhere else that mattered. Paulson was twenty-five, ROTC, six months in-country before the world ended, and he gave the order with the particular confidence of a man who had learned command from a textbook and had not yet encountered the chapter about when the textbook stops working.

"Sergeant Morrow. Take your section to the south interior fence line. Establish a defensive position at the Sector B gate. Hold until relieved."

Morrow looked at Paulson. Looked past him at the south interior fence — chain-link, six feet, the same fence that had separated the military side from the civilian blocks since the compound was established. On the other side of that fence, Sector B was dark. The power had been cut to the civilian blocks two hours ago when the generators were redirected to the armory and command. Whatever was in Sector B now was in there without light, without guard posts, without the institutional machinery that had been the only thing between the civilians and the thing outside the wire.

The thing outside the wire was no longer only outside the wire.

"Sir," Morrow said. "The civilian sectors are compromised. The south interior fence is chain-link with no reinforcement. There is no fallback position between that fence and the motor pool. If we establish at the Sector B gate, we're holding a screen door against an unknown number of hostiles with no extraction plan and no fire support."

Paulson's jaw worked. The confidence flickered — not gone, but tested, the textbook encountering the chapter it hadn't read. "The order is to hold the south interior line. Command needs time to complete the evacuation staging."

Morrow heard it. The sentence underneath the sentence. Hold the line so the command block can load vehicles and leave north. The south interior fence wasn't a defensive position. It was a delay. A speed bump made of soldiers, placed between the command block and whatever was coming through the civilian sectors, designed to buy minutes for people who would not be there when the minutes ran out.

"Copy, sir," Morrow said.

He turned and walked toward the motor pool.

The walk took seven minutes. He used each one. The compound around him was a thing he had spent his career inside — the institutional structure, the chain, the architecture of command that told you where to stand and who to listen to and what direction to face. He had needed it the way he needed structure, the way the structure was the water and without it the mechanism of Dale Morrow did not know how to run.

The mechanism was running now on something else. Not rebellion — Morrow didn't have rebellion in him the way Vasquez did, the moral clarity that made crossing a line feel like arriving at the right side of it. What Morrow had was a different calculation: the institution that gave orders no longer existed in the form that made the orders legitimate. The chain of command was a chain, and the links had been replaced by strangers, and the strangers were ordering him to die for an evacuation he wouldn't be part of.

His people. His call.

He found Reyes first. Reyes was where Reyes always was — at the edge of things, the periphery that was both his position and his nature. He was standing at the motor pool fence, watching the tree line to the east with the unhurried attention of a man who had been watching tree lines for most of his adult life and had learned that the important things happened at the margins of what other people were looking at.

"We're leaving," Morrow said.

Reyes turned his head. The assessment took two seconds — Morrow's face, Morrow's posture, the sidearm on Morrow's hip and the direction Morrow had come from and the direction Morrow was not going back to. Two seconds. The read completed. The conclusion reached.

"When," Reyes said.

"Now."

Reyes nodded. Not agreement — acknowledgment. The distinction mattered to Reyes. He did not agree or disagree with decisions. He assessed them, determined their operational validity, and acted accordingly. This one was operationally valid. He picked up his rifle and fell in.

Cutler was at the motor pool, which was where Morrow expected him to be because the motor pool was where the consolidation had put him and because the motor pool was where Cutler would have put himself regardless. He was behind the maintenance bay, crouched next to a Humvee that Morrow had not seen in the regular vehicle rotation.

"Cutler."

Cutler looked up. His hands were dirty — they were always dirty, the permanent condition of a man whose relationship to the world was conducted through machinery. He wiped his palms on his thighs, the gesture he made before touching anything mechanical, except this time it was the reverse — finishing a job rather than starting one.

"I know," Cutler said. "Been working on it."

Morrow looked at the Humvee. It was an M998 — standard, unarmored, the workhorse of every military installation he'd ever served at. But this one was different in the ways that Cutler made things different: the engine running clean, the idle steady, the fuel gauge reading full. Two jerry cans strapped to the rear cargo rack with ratchet ties. A field radio mounted to the dash — AN/VRC-series, the kind that could reach twenty miles on a good day and farther at night when the skip was right. Behind the front seats, stacked with the organized density of a man who planned the way he maintained engines — MREs, water cans, a tool kit in a canvas roll, ammunition boxes, a medical kit that looked like it had been assembled from three different sources.

"How long have you been building this?" Morrow asked.

Cutler stood up. Wiped his hands again. "Since the generator failed on the east side. Four days."

Four days. While Morrow had been processing the consolidation, navigating the command structure, watching the institutional machinery grind toward the conclusion it was grinding toward — Cutler had been preparing the alternative. Not because Cutler had decided to leave. Because Cutler had decided to be ready, which was the mechanic's version of the same thing, the preparation that was itself the decision, the wrench that said what the mouth didn't.

"Fuel?"

"Full tank plus twenty gallons in the cans. That's four hundred miles if we don't push it."

"Radio?"

"Tuned and tested this morning. Military frequencies and civilian shortwave both. It'll reach."

Morrow looked at Cutler. At the Humvee. At the preparation that represented four days of a man who didn't speak about decisions but built them out of metal and fuel and forethought.

"Get it to the east gate," Morrow said. "Five minutes."

"I'll be there in three."

Webb was the last. Morrow found him at the north sector barracks — the holding detail, the place where spare bodies went when the institution had used up the structure they belonged to and hadn't yet found a new structure to put them in. Webb was standing at the doorway with his rifle and the look that Jesse Hutchins had identified from fifty yards away in the first week — the half-second vacancy, the nineteen-year-old gaze that went somewhere else and came back without bringing anything with it.

"Webb."

Webb looked at him. The vacancy cleared — not fully, not the way it cleared when Webb was actively receiving orders and the orders gave him a surface to stand on. But enough. Enough to see Morrow and register that Morrow was there and that Morrow being there meant something different from the last six hours of standing at a doorway waiting for someone to tell him what to do.

"Sergeant."

"We're leaving the compound. All of us — you, me, Reyes, Cutler. I'm not ordering you. I'm telling you what I'm doing and I'm asking you to come."

Webb was still for a moment. The stillness was not the vacancy — it was something underneath it, the nineteen-year-old processing a thing that the forty-eight hours of his military career before the world ended had not prepared him for. A sergeant telling him that the institution was over and the choice was his and the choice was now.

"Where," Webb said.

"East. Away from the perimeter. Away from all of this."

"Is that—" Webb stopped. Restarted. "Is there somewhere to go?"

Morrow looked at him. At the nineteen-year-old face that still had the unfinished quality of someone who had not yet become whoever he was going to be. At the rifle held with the grip that training had given him and the posture that training had given him and the vacancy that the field had given him, the thing that training didn't cover.

"I don't know," Morrow said. "But there's nothing to stay for."

Webb picked up his pack. He didn't say anything else. He fell in behind Morrow the way he had fallen in behind Morrow since the convoy — not because the order was there but because the man was there, and the man was the last structure that hadn't dissolved.

They crossed the interior yard toward the motor pool. The compound was coming apart around them — vehicles moving without coordination, soldiers running to positions or from positions, the radio traffic a wall of overlapping voices that had lost the clean procedural cadence of command and become the sound of an institution exceeding its own tolerances. A burst of gunfire from the south interior fence — Paulson's position, the chain-link line that Morrow had been ordered to hold. He did not turn toward it.

At the command block, a figure in the doorway. Morrow saw him in the peripheral vision that a career of soldiering had tuned to register movement at the edges — the posture, the bearing, the particular uprightness that was not performance but architecture.

Greer.

He was in the doorway of the command block, one hand on the frame, half-turned toward the interior where the command staff was doing whatever the command staff was doing in the last hours of Checkpoint Harlan. His face was lit by the overhead bulb — the last powered section of the compound, the generators feeding the command block while everything else went dark — and the face was the face Morrow had known since the convoy. Not the road Greer, who had sat in a small room and told the truth about Mitchell and Pawlowski. Not the Harlan Greer, who had been absorbed back into the institution and moved through the compound in jeeps without stopping. Something between them. The face of a man who knew what was happening and had chosen to be inside it because the inside was where his wiring told him to stand.

Their eyes met across thirty yards of open ground. The distance was too far for words and the right distance for what passed between them — the recognition, the assessment, the knowledge that two men who respected each other were walking in opposite directions and that neither direction was wrong in the terms of the man walking it.

Morrow gave a nod. Slight. Not a request. Not an apology. The acknowledgment of a thing that didn't need language because the language would have been smaller than the thing.

Greer returned it. One nod. The same weight. Then he turned back into the command block and the doorway was empty.

Morrow kept walking.

*   *   *

The motor pool was controlled chaos — vehicles being loaded for the command's northern evacuation, soldiers moving crates and equipment with the urgent imprecision of people who knew they were running out of time but not how much time they were running out of. Nobody was watching the east side of the lot. Nobody was watching much of anything with the kind of attention that would have noticed five people converging on a Humvee that wasn't part of the evacuation manifest.

Cutler had the vehicle at the east gate. Engine running, the idle clean and steady, the sound of a machine that had been maintained by someone who understood that maintenance was a form of loyalty — to the vehicle, to the people who would need it, to the future that the present was trying to destroy.

Reyes was already in the back, rifle across his knees, eyes on the tree line. Webb climbed in beside him without being told where to sit. Morrow reached the driver's door and stopped.

Vasquez was there.

She was standing at the passenger side of the Humvee with her sidearm holstered and her patrol vest on and the look on her face that Morrow had seen exactly once before — on the road, the night Reyes disclosed the forty-second reanimation window, the look of a woman who had processed something irreversible and arrived at the other side of it.

"Sergeant," she said.

"Sergeant," he said.

The exchange was two words and contained the entire conversation. Two NCOs. Fifteen years and fourteen years, respectively. Both inside the machine. Both walking out. The reasons were different — hers moral, his structural — and the difference didn't matter because they were standing at the same vehicle pointing in the same direction.

"The family went east," Vasquez said. "County roads, away from the perimeter. They have a shortwave on 7.285 megahertz."

Morrow processed it. East. The same direction the tactical math pointed — the perimeter collapsing south and west, the military consolidating north, east the only vector that bought distance from all three failure modes. Rural. Small towns. Time.

"How do you know?"

Vasquez touched her breast pocket. "Diane left a note."

He didn't ask what the note said. He didn't need to. The note was a frequency and a direction and the fact of Diane Hutchins leaving a door open for the woman who'd crossed lines for her family, and the frequency was the thread that would lead them to the people who had left Harlan hours ago in two vehicles heading east on a county road with no center line.

"Mount up," Morrow said.

Vasquez got in the passenger seat. Morrow got behind the wheel. He checked the mirrors — a habit, a discipline, the structure reasserting itself in the small physical rituals that survived even when the institution that created them didn't. Reyes in the back left, rifle oriented toward the window. Cutler in the back center, hands on his knees, already looking at the dashboard gauges with the evaluative attention of a man who would know by sound and vibration if anything was wrong before the instruments told him. Webb on the right, pack between his feet, his face turned toward the window and the compound beyond it.

Morrow pulled out of the motor pool. East gate — the vehicle entrance that led to the perimeter road, which led to the county roads, which led to the dark. The gate was open. The soldiers who would have manned it were at the south interior fence or the armory or gone.

The perimeter road ran along the outside of the compound's eastern fence. In the headlights — Morrow drove with headlights because driving without them on a road that might contain the dead was a faster way to die than being seen — the fence scrolled past on the left. Through it, the compound. The civilian blocks, dark, the tents still standing in their rows like the set of a play after the audience had left. The armory, lit, vehicles loading. The command block. The staging area where the family had been hours ago, where Gary Hutchins had broken the chain on the fence gate and Marcus had arrived in a stolen Suburban and ten people had driven into the dark.

The road curved north, then east, away from the compound. The fence ended. The tree line began — Kentucky hardwoods, oak and hickory, their canopy dense and dark against a sky that was the wrong color, the purple-black that had been the sky's color for days, the stars filtered through whatever was in the atmosphere that made the light wrong.

Something in the road. Morrow saw it in the headlights and his foot moved to the brake before his mind had fully categorized it — a figure, standing in the center of the blacktop, facing the wrong direction. Not facing them. Facing the compound. Drawn by the sound of the engines and the gunfire and the institutional collapse, drawn the way they were always drawn, by input and proximity and the absence of anything inside them that could choose not to respond.

It had been a soldier. The uniform was visible in the headlights — BDUs, boots, the silhouette recognizable even from behind. The posture was wrong in the way it was always wrong, the weight distributed on joints that were no longer being managed by the system that had built them for walking. One arm hung at an angle that was not injury. The head was tilted.

Morrow didn't stop. He pulled the wheel left, took the Humvee onto the gravel shoulder, and passed the figure at thirty miles an hour with two feet of clearance. In the mirror, the figure turned — slow, mechanical, the head tracking the sound of the engine the way a dish tracks a signal — and then the headlights left it behind and it was dark and the road was empty again.

Nobody in the vehicle said anything.

The road went east. Two lanes, cracked, the blacktop scarred by years of Kentucky weather and the passage of vehicles that had been using it since before any of this started. The tree line pressed in on both sides, the canopy meeting overhead in places so that the headlights hit green before finding road. No houses. No mailboxes. No center line.

Morrow drove. The Humvee handled the road the way military vehicles handled roads — without complaint, without grace, the suspension absorbing the cracks and the weeds and the neglect with the indifference of a machine built for worse. The fuel gauge read full. The radio on the dash was lit, the frequencies preset, the field of static and silence that was the world's voice now.

In the back seat, Reyes watched.

He watched the way he always watched — systematically, the scan moving from the road to the tree line to the mirrors to the road again, the sequence that his body ran without his mind's permission because the body had been doing it longer than the mind had been thinking about it. The tree line was dark. The road was dark. The sky was the wrong color. The Humvee's headlights cut a tunnel through the night that closed behind them as they passed, and the world outside the tunnel was a thing that existed only by inference — the sound of the trees, the smell of the air through the cracked window, the sulfur-copper smell that had been the background frequency of everything for weeks.

He was not thinking about Harlan. The compound was behind them and behind was a direction that Reyes had trained himself to process as data, not emotion. What was behind: an institution that had failed. What was ahead: unknown. The space between those two facts was the space in which Reyes operated — the periphery, the margin, the edge of things where the important information lived.

The road curved. The headlights swept the blacktop and the shoulders and the tree line and came back to center.

Reyes saw the tracks.

Tire marks on the asphalt. Fresh — the rubber still dark against the pale surface, not yet scuffed by wind or dusted over by the fine grit that the atmosphere deposited on everything now. Two sets. Two vehicles, one slightly wider than the other, both heading east. The tracks held the road's curves with the discipline of vehicles driven at moderate speed by drivers who were paying attention — not fleeing, not racing, but moving with the deliberate pace of people who knew where they were going even if where they were going was only a direction.

He filed it. The way he filed everything — without announcement, without reaction, the information entering the system and being held until it had operational value. Two vehicles. Heading east. Hours ahead, based on the track degradation. The family had a Ford Bronco and a Chevy Suburban. Two vehicles.

He didn't say anything. Morrow was driving. Vasquez was looking ahead, the note in her pocket, the frequency in her head. Cutler was listening to the engine. Webb was against the door, the vacancy in his face softened by something that might have been exhaustion or might have been the first thing past exhaustion, the place where the body stopped performing and started just being.

The tracks went east. The road went east. The Humvee followed both.

Reyes watched the dark, and the dark gave him nothing back except the road and the trees and the tire marks of people who had passed this way before them, heading toward whatever was ahead of them, which was the same thing that was ahead of everyone now.

The unknown. The east. The morning that was coming whether they were ready for it or not.

He settled into the seat. Adjusted the rifle across his knees. Let the scan run.

Behind them, the sky above Harlan glowed a dull orange, the color of a place that was burning from the inside. Ahead of them, the dark.

The road went on.
